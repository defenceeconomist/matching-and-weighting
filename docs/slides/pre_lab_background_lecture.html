From Report to Labs

Background Lecture Before the Matching Labs

Author

Luke Heley

Published

March 6, 2026

1 Why This Lecture Exists

  • It follows the main report rather than replacing it.
  • It turns the report’s argument into a reusable lab checklist.
  • It gives the group a shared standard before the datasets change.
Frame this as a bridge session. The audience has already seen the main report, so the job here is not to repeat every method detail. The job is to stabilize the design logic they should carry into the labs.

2 What The Main Report Already Established

  • Start with the causal question and estimand, not the software.
  • Judge balance together with overlap, retention, and weight concentration.
  • Treat matching as one option inside a wider quasi-experimental toolkit.
  • Read every adjusted estimate as design-contingent rather than automatic.
This slide should sound familiar. Use it to remind the room that the labs are extensions of the same argument, not separate exercises with a different standard.

3 What The Labs Are For

  • Re-run the same design-first workflow on new datasets.
  • See how method costs change when overlap is weaker or the data structure changes.
  • Practice defending design choices out loud, not just running code to completion.
Make the pedagogic point explicit: the labs are for judgement. A technically correct script that skips the design argument is still a weak answer.

4 The Non-Negotiable Workflow

  1. Define treatment, outcome, covariates, and estimand.
  2. Inspect raw imbalance before adjusting anything.
  3. Use exact matching as a transparency check where feasible.
  4. Compare CEM and entropy balancing on the same target.
  5. Estimate effects only after the design is defensible.
Deliver this as the lab contract. If participants jump straight to the outcome model or the final coefficient, bring them back to this sequence.

5 Four Questions To Ask In Every Lab

  • Who is the treated group we are trying to learn about?
  • What makes a control unit comparable before treatment?
  • How much design cost are we paying to improve comparability?
  • After adjustment, what population does the estimate actually describe?
These four questions are useful because they work across all three datasets. They also surface estimand drift early, before people get attached to a particular estimate.

6 Reading Balance Without Fooling Yourself

  • Standardized mean differences near zero are useful, not sufficient.
  • Love plots help show whether imbalance is broad or concentrated.
  • Continuous covariates also need distribution checks, not just mean checks.
  • Good balance on the wrong covariates is still a weak design.
Slow down on the last point. Many learners assume a neat balance table proves design quality. Stress that the covariate set still has to be causally defensible and measured before treatment.

7 Reading Design Cost

Design move What improves What you must still check
Matching Visible comparability rules Who got dropped and whether the treated target narrowed
Weighting Retains the original rows Effective sample size and whether a few units now dominate
This is the cleanest way to explain the matching-versus-weighting difference before the hands-on work starts. Matching makes sample restriction visible. Weighting keeps rows but can hide information loss inside the weights.

8 Method Choice In Lab Terms

  • Exact matching: best as a feasibility check with a clear audit trail. Watch for continuous differences that still sit outside the match.
  • CEM: best matched-sample compromise when you want visible design rules. Watch for cutpoints that quietly redefine who remains comparable.
  • Entropy balancing: best when treated retention matters and mean balance must be strong. Watch for extreme weights and a collapsing control effective sample size.
Keep this practical rather than abstract. The point is not to rank methods universally. The point is to teach what each method is buying and where the hidden cost tends to appear.

9 Common Failure Modes

  • Choosing a method before naming the estimand.
  • Pulling in variables that are post-treatment or ambiguous in timing.
  • Reporting the adjusted effect without reporting balance and overlap.
  • Treating nominal retention as proof that the target population stayed intact.
  • Ignoring weight dispersion because the balance table looks perfect.
Use this slide to inoculate the room against the most common shortcuts. If you want to make it concrete, mention that entropy balancing can keep every row while still reducing the effective control sample sharply.

10 How The Lab Bundle Is Sequenced

Lab What it is best for
Black Politicians Structural imbalance, thin comparability, and method fragility
NSW + CPS Benchmark Comparing observational designs against an experimental reference
Titanic Compact teaching on support cells, exact matching, and subclassification
Explain the logic of the sequence. Black Politicians is the best immediate follow-on from the report because it keeps the design-first comparison visible. NSW plus CPS adds a benchmark question. Titanic is the short appendix when you want a simpler classroom example.

11 What A Defensible Lab Answer Looks Like

  • It states the estimand before any adjustment output.
  • It explains why the chosen covariates belong in the design.
  • It reports raw and adjusted diagnostics on the same scale.
  • It names the retention or weighting cost directly.
  • It says what causal claim is still out of reach.
This is the marking guide in slide form. End by reminding participants that a careful negative conclusion, such as “the data do not support this comparison cleanly enough,” is a good answer if the diagnostics point that way.

12 Transition To The Labs

  • Start with the Black Politicians lab.
  • Move to the NSW + CPS benchmark lab when you want an external reference point.
  • Use the Titanic teaching lab for the shortest support-focused exercise.
  • Keep the main report open as the design reference.
Close with a simple handoff. Tell the audience which page to open first and what standard to keep in mind: define the target, inspect the raw comparison, then decide whether the design cost is acceptable.
