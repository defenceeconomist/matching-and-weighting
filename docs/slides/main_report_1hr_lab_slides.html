Delivering the Main Report in a 1-Hour Lab

Facilitator Deck Aligned to the Teaching Aid

Author

Luke Heley

Published

March 6, 2026

1 Session Goal

By the end of the hour, participants should be able to say:

Matching and weighting are useful design tools, but they have to be judged by the tradeoff they impose between balance, overlap, retention, and weight concentration.

Use this as the anchor sentence for the whole session. If discussion wanders into software details or isolated estimates, return to this line.

2 What This Lab Is For

  • deliver the report’s main messages, not every technical detail
  • train design judgement rather than software mimicry
  • prepare the room for the follow-on labs
Open by lowering the pressure to cover everything. The point is to teach the report’s logic well enough that participants can carry it into later exercises.

3 60-Minute Flow

Time Focus
0-5 Opening frame
5-15 Core report argument
15-25 Design-first workflow
25-40 Method comparison
40-50 Interpreting the worked example
50-60 Discussion and handoff
Set expectations up front. This slide gives you permission to move the room along rather than turning the lab into a line-by-line report walkthrough.

4 Opening Frame

  • This is a lab about design judgement, not button-pushing.
  • The methods solve related but not identical design problems.
  • The key question is not “which method wins?”
  • The key question is “what design cost are we willing to defend?”
Ask what usually gets reported first in matching write-ups: design or estimate. Use that to surface the report’s critique of outcome-first presentation.

5 Three Messages To Land Early

  1. Define the estimand before choosing the method.
  2. Treat matching and weighting as design-stage tools.
  3. Read balance together with retention and effective sample size.
If participants only remember three things, make it these. Repeat them at least twice during the hour.

6 The Design-First Workflow

  1. Identify the target as the ATT.
  2. Define the treated group as programme participants.
  3. Name the defensible pre-treatment covariates.
  4. Inspect raw imbalance before any adjustment.
  5. Compare exact, CEM, and entropy balancing on the same target.
Keep returning to the same ATT target. That prevents the methods discussion from becoming a set of disconnected technique demos.

7 What The Pre-Match Section Is Doing

  • It shows the untreated group is not automatically a credible stand-in.
  • It reveals where the design burden is heaviest.
  • It tells you what kinds of costs you should expect later.

Ask:

  • Which pre-treatment differences worry you most?
  • What happens if overlap is weak on prior earnings?
Use the report’s pre-match diagnostics to make the comparison problem visible. Do not rush past this slide. It sets up why the methods sections matter.

8 Exact Matching

  • strongest audit trail
  • easiest method to explain in plain language
  • often too rigid once important continuous covariates matter

Teaching line:

clear rules, but often too rigid

Stress that exact matching is valuable here as a teaching contrast. Its clarity helps, but it cannot carry the full design on its own in the lalonde example.

9 Coarsened Exact Matching

  • keeps matching logic visible
  • brings continuous covariates into the design through bins
  • gives the clearest matched-sample compromise in the report

Teaching line:

auditable compromise, but dependent on cutpoints

Explain that CEM is strong precisely because its compromises stay visible. Then note that those visible cutpoints are real design choices, not neutral defaults.

10 Entropy Balancing

  • preserves the original treated group
  • achieves very strong observed mean balance
  • moves more of the design cost into the weights

Teaching line:

strong balance, but cost moved into the weights

This is the moment to introduce effective sample size as the weighting analogue of sample loss. Make the cost concrete rather than abstract.

11 What The Worked Example Shows

  • All three adjusted designs point in the same direction.
  • That directional stability does not remove the design tradeoffs.
  • The represented population shifts as pruning or weight concentration increases.
Push back against the lazy conclusion that positive estimates across methods settle the problem. The report’s point is that design costs still determine what the estimate means.

12 Qualified Method Choice

  • Entropy balancing is strongest if preserving the original treated population is the priority.
  • CEM is strongest if you want an auditable matched-sample compromise.
  • Exact matching is easiest to teach, but not the strongest overall design here.
State this as a qualified judgement, not a universal rule. Tie each choice back to the causal question and the acceptable design cost.

13 Discussion Prompts

  • What is the report’s main message in one sentence?
  • Which design cost worries you most in lalonde?
  • What would make you reject matching entirely in a real evaluation?
Use these prompts to close the loop. Participants should leave by articulating the report’s argument themselves, not just hearing you state it.

14 If Time Runs Short

Keep only:

  1. core report argument
  2. pre-match diagnostics
  3. exact vs CEM vs entropy balancing
  4. treatment-effect summary and limitations
  5. handoff to the next lab

Drop:

  • detailed code walkthroughs
  • robustness details
  • extended tuning discussion
This slide is mainly for you as facilitator. If the session slips, protect the report’s central logic and cut the implementation detail first.

15 Handoff To The Next Materials

  • Teaching aid page
  • Main report

  • Black Politicians lab
End with a clear next step. The room should know where to go next and what standard to carry forward: define the target, diagnose the raw comparison, then decide whether the design cost is defensible.
