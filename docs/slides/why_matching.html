Why We Do Matching

A causal-inference deck on confounding, comparability, and design

2026-03-09

Session Promise

This short deck explains why matching exists before it explains any specific matching algorithm.

  • start from the counterfactual problem
  • show how confounding distorts raw comparisons
  • explain why matching is a design-stage attempt to improve comparability
  • keep the limits explicit throughout (Cunningham 2021a; Huntington-Klein 2021)

Start With The Fundamental Problem

  • each unit has a treated potential outcome and an untreated potential outcome
  • we only observe one of them for any given unit
  • the missing outcome is the counterfactual
  • causal inference is the problem of constructing a credible stand-in for that missing outcome (Cunningham 2021a)

Why We Need A Comparison Group

Observed outcome

What happened under the treatment state actually received.

Missing outcome

What would have happened under the alternative treatment state.

Design task

Build a comparison group that is credible enough to stand in for the missing outcome.

Best Benchmark: Randomization

  • if treatment is randomized, the treated and untreated groups are comparable in expectation
  • that is why RCTs are the clearest benchmark for causal comparison quality
  • but RCTs are a benchmark, not magic: attrition, noncompliance, and external validity still matter (Heiss 2026)

Why Matching Enters The Story

  • in many policy settings, treatment was not randomized
  • the comparison group is therefore not automatically credible
  • matching matters because it is a second-best design repair when stronger design features are unavailable
  • the job is to recover the best comparison the data can still defend (Cunningham 2021a; Huntington-Klein 2021)

Dataset We Use: lalonde

  • the lab and report use MatchIt::lalonde, built from the National Supported Work training study with a non-experimental comparison group
  • this dataset became a benchmark because LaLonde showed that many non-experimental estimators missed the experimental training effect (LaLonde 1986)
  • Dehejia and Wahba made it a matching case study by showing that better design choices could recover more credible estimates (Dehejia and Wahba 1999)
  • Abadie and Imbens kept it central in the matching literature because it is a disciplined test of how matching estimators behave when the benchmark is known (Abadie and Imbens 2011)

We use lalonde because it is not just convenient. It is a benchmark dataset for asking whether an observational design can get back to something close to the experimental answer.

Why Raw Comparisons Mislead

  • in observational data, treatment is usually related to prior characteristics
  • the treated group may differ from the untreated group before treatment starts
  • a raw difference in outcomes therefore combines:
    • the effect of treatment
    • the effect of pre-existing differences
  • that mixture is the basic confounding problem

The Confounder Problem

  • a confounder is a common cause of treatment and outcome
  • confounders create backdoor paths between treatment and outcome
  • if those paths remain open, the observed association is not a clean causal effect (Cunningham 2021a; Huntington-Klein 2021)

Backdoor Path

Figure 1

  • this is the core confounding case: D <- X -> Y
  • Prior need is a common cause of treatment and outcome
  • the raw treated-control gap mixes treatment effects with selection (Cunningham, Scott 2025)

What This Means For Design

  • if treated units differ from controls on Prior need1, the raw outcome gap is confounded
  • part of the observed gap then reflects pre-treatment selection, not just treatment
  • the direction of bias depends on whether Prior need raises or lowers the outcome
  • a credible design must block the backdoor path by conditioning on the right pre-treatment variables
  • this is the bridge from causal logic to matching logic (Cunningham 2021a)

  1. Prior need means baseline disadvantage or need that existed before treatment, such as poorer health, lower prior earnings, or higher case severity.

Block The Backdoor

Figure 2

  • once the confounder is conditioned on, the remaining open path is the causal path from treatment to outcome
  • regression, matching, subclassification, and weighting all aim at this same design goal
  • the methods differ in implementation, not in the underlying causal target (Cunningham, Scott 2025)

Matching In One Sentence

  • matching tries to compare treated units with untreated units that are similar on relevant observed pre-treatment covariates
  • the aim is to make treatment status less entangled with baseline differences
  • matching is therefore a design tool for reducing observed confounding, not a guarantee of identification

Why Match?

Matching is useful because imbalance creates model dependence.

  • imbalance -> model dependence -> researcher discretion -> bias
  • raw mismatch forces the outcome model to extrapolate
  • extrapolation creates analyst discretion about specification
  • analyst discretion can move the estimate even when the causal story is unchanged
  • matching trims or down-weights the least credible comparisons first (Heiss 2026; Ho et al. 2007)

Why Matching Appeals In Practice

Visible comparisons

The analyst has to show who is being compared to whom.

Less hidden extrapolation

The design leans less on functional-form assumptions alone.

Explicit diagnostics

Balance, overlap, retention, and weights become reportable design costs.

Design In Two Steps

1. Preprocess the design

  • define the estimand
  • choose covariates from the assignment story
  • inspect imbalance and overlap
  • match or weight before estimating

2. Estimate on the redesigned data

  • use the matched sample or weights as the design
  • estimate the treatment effect after preprocessing
  • report uncertainty that reflects the adjusted design

From Subclassification To Matching

  • subclassification is the clearest starting point: compare treated and untreated units within the same covariate-defined strata
  • exact matching is the strict version of that logic
  • approximate matching relaxes exact equality when exact strata become too sparse
  • the underlying idea stays the same: compare like with like (Cunningham 2021b)

The Curse Of Dimensionality

  • once many covariates matter at once, exact strata proliferate
  • some cells become tiny or empty
  • overlap disappears for parts of the treated group
  • matching methods exist because the causal logic is simple, but the data rarely support simple exact comparisons everywhere (Cunningham 2021b)

Good Controls, Not All Controls

  • matching should target plausible pre-treatment confounders
  • not every available baseline variable belongs in the design
  • the right question is causal: which variables help block confounding without opening new problems?
  • this is where causal reasoning matters most
  • a pre-treatment common cause belongs in the design
  • a collider does not become safe just because it was measured before treatment
  • a post-treatment mediator does not belong in the matching design (Cunningham, Scott 2025)

Collider Warning

Figure 3

  • a collider is different from a confounder
  • if you condition on Movie star, you can create a spurious association between Talent and Beauty
  • this is why not every pre-treatment variable belongs in the design (Cunningham, Scott 2025)

What Matching Is Buying

Problem What matching tries to improve
Baseline imbalance More comparable treated and control units
Opaque regression dependence A clearer comparison rule
Weak visibility on overlap Explicit evidence on where support fails
Hidden design costs Retention and weighting costs that can be reported plainly

Matching And Weighting Are Siblings

  • both are design-stage responses to the same comparison problem
  • matching changes who remains in the analytic sample
  • weighting changes how much each unit counts in the final comparison
  • many matching procedures can also be understood as weighted averages rather than as mysterious algorithms (Huntington-Klein 2021; Heiss 2026)

One Logic, Several Implementations

Regression

Compares outcomes conditional on observed covariates.

Matching

Builds more comparable treated-control pairs or samples first.

Stratification and weights

Take within-cell differences and aggregate them with weights.

All are trying to estimate causal contrasts after blocking backdoor paths with observed covariates.

Matching Is Still About Assumptions

Conditional independence

No important unmeasured confounders remain after conditioning.

Common support

Comparable treated and untreated units must exist.

Balance

Observed covariates should actually look similar after adjustment.

Stable estimand

The design should still answer the question you started with.

What Matching Cannot Do

  • it cannot fix omitted confounding
  • it cannot rescue a design with no overlap
  • it cannot justify bad controls
  • it cannot turn observational data into randomized data
  • it should not be the default when a stronger design is available

When Not To Match

  • do not default to matching when a credible RDD, DiD, or randomized design is available
  • do not force matching when overlap is so weak that only severe pruning or extreme weights make the comparison work
  • do not pretend a matched design answers the original question if the estimand has drifted to a narrower subgroup

Why We Still Do It

  • many important programme and policy questions are observational
  • regression alone can hide the comparison problem rather than expose it
  • matching forces the analyst to defend the counterfactual more directly
  • even when imperfect, it can make a weak design more transparent and less model-dependent

Practical Standard

  • define the estimand first
  • choose covariates from the assignment story
  • inspect raw imbalance and overlap
  • match or weight as a design step
  • estimate only after the adjusted design is credible enough to defend
  • follow the main design with uncertainty checks and sensitivity checks
  • report the remaining limitations directly

What The Lab Focuses On

We focus on

  • exact matching as the transparency benchmark
  • coarsened exact matching as the main approximate matching method
  • entropy balancing as the weighting comparison

We do not center

  • nearest-neighbor propensity-score matching in the core lab
  • optimal, full, genetic, or other advanced matching variants
  • the full MatchIt method menu on a first pass

This narrower scope keeps the design rule visible. For the broader implementation menu, see the MatchIt documentation.

Closing Line

We do matching to reduce observed confounding by building a more credible comparison group, while remaining honest that causality still depends on assumptions the data cannot prove.
