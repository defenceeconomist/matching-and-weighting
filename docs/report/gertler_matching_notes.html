1 Purpose

These notes summarize Chapter 8 (“Matching”) in Impact Evaluation in Practice using the recently extracted export in data/evidencebase_exports/gertler_ch8/. They are meant to capture the chapter’s actual argument and worked examples rather than only the bookmark structure.

The aim here is to capture:

  • what matching is doing conceptually
  • the specific conditions Gertler et al. place on credible matching
  • how the HISP worked example is used to motivate propensity-score matching
  • where the chapter says matching should be combined with stronger designs

3 Executive Summary

  • Matching = build an artificial comparison group from untreated units that resemble treated units on observed baseline characteristics.
  • Exact matching is conceptually clean but quickly runs into the curse of dimensionality.
  • Propensity-score matching is the chapter’s main practical workaround.
  • The non-negotiables are baseline covariates, common support, and the absence of important unobserved selection.
  • The HISP example shows that richer baseline covariates produce more credible matches.
  • The chapter’s strongest practical advice is to combine matching with difference-in-differences when baseline outcomes exist.

4 Study Notes

4.1 Constructing an Artificial Comparison Group

Main idea:

  • Matching is for settings where treatment assignment was not randomized and there is no clean eligibility rule to lean on.
  • The evaluator tries to find untreated units that look as similar as possible to treated units before treatment.

Why this matters:

  • The whole method is trying to approximate the counterfactual using design choices inside the data.
  • The target is not “similarity” in a vague sense. The target is similarity on characteristics that help explain enrollment.

What to remember:

  • Matching is a selection-process method.
  • The quality of the comparison group depends on whether the data capture the variables that drove take-up.

4.2 Key Concept: Observed Characteristics Only

Main idea:

  • The chapter’s short definition is that matching uses large data sets and statistical techniques to construct the best possible comparison group based on observed characteristics.

Implications:

  • Matching can only adjust for what is measured.
  • Bigger, richer baseline data sets help.
  • Unobserved confounding remains the core vulnerability.

Watch for:

  • The chapter’s exact-matching example is there to show why this gets hard fast once many relevant covariates matter at the same time.

5 Propensity-Score Matching

5.1 Why the Chapter Moves Beyond Exact Matching

Main idea:

  • Propensity-score matching is introduced as the fix for the curse of dimensionality.
  • Instead of exact agreement on every covariate, units are matched on estimated enrollment probabilities.

What to remember:

  • The propensity score is a design tool, not the estimand.
  • The point is to produce a matched sample with better covariate comparability.

5.2 Baseline Covariates Are Non-Negotiable

Main idea:

  • Only pre-treatment observed characteristics should go into the propensity score.

Why:

  • Post-treatment variables may already contain treatment effects.
  • Matching on them can make treated and control units look artificially similar for the wrong reason.

What to remember:

  • Pre-treatment measurement is part of the identification logic, not a nice-to-have.

5.3 Common Support

Main idea:

  • Matching only works where treated and untreated units overlap in propensity-score space.

Why this matters:

  • No overlap means no credible counterfactual for some treated units.
  • In that case the estimate only applies to the subset on common support.

Watch for:

  • weak overlap at the tails
  • forced matches that look mathematical but not substantive
  • sample restriction changing the target population

Evaluator workflow in the chapter:

  1. Obtain representative, comparable data on enrolled and nonenrolled units.
  2. Pool the samples and estimate enrollment probabilities from observed characteristics.
  3. Restrict to the region of common support.
  4. Match each enrolled unit to similar nonenrolled units.
  5. Compare outcomes for treated units and their matched comparisons.
  6. Average those unit-level impacts to obtain an overall effect estimate.

5.4 What Matching Can and Cannot Do

Recurring cautions:

  • matching only accounts for observed characteristics
  • matching should not rely on post-treatment covariates
  • the choice of matching variables matters most when those variables actually determine enrollment

Bottom line:

  • Good software cannot rescue a bad covariate set or weak knowledge of the assignment process.

6 Combining Matching with Other Methods

6.1 Matched Difference-in-Differences

Main idea:

  • If baseline outcomes exist, the chapter prefers matching plus DiD over matching alone.

Why this matters:

  • Matching handles comparability on observables.
  • DiD helps remove bias from unobserved differences that are fixed over time.

Workflow:

  1. Match on observed baseline characteristics.
  2. Compute before-after outcome changes for treated units.
  3. Compute before-after outcome changes for their matched comparisons.
  4. Subtract the two changes.
  5. Average the resulting double differences.

What to remember:

  • Matching alone is relatively weak.
  • Matching plus DiD is stronger because it adds a second identification layer.

6.2 Synthetic Control

Main idea:

  • Synthetic control is the single-treated-unit extension of the chapter’s matching logic.

What it does:

  • It builds a weighted synthetic comparison unit rather than choosing one untreated match.

What to remember:

  • It needs a long pre-treatment time series.
  • The Basque Country example shows why naive comparisons can fail when pre-treatment determinants already differ.

7 HISP Worked Example

7.1 Enrollment Model

Main idea:

  • HISP is the chapter’s worked example for how matching is actually implemented.

Baseline predictors used in the richer specification:

  • age of the household head and spouse
  • head and spouse education
  • whether the household head is female
  • indigenous status
  • household size
  • dirt floor
  • bathroom access
  • hectares of land
  • distance to hospital

What the table suggests:

  • HISP appears to be targeted toward poorer and less-educated households.
  • Enrollment is more likely for indigenous households, larger households, households with dirt floors, and households farther from hospitals.
  • Enrollment is less likely for older, more educated households and households with bathrooms or more land.

7.2 Common Support and Match Construction

Main idea:

  • With the full covariate set, the chapter reports full common support for enrolled households.
  • The example uses nearest-neighbor matching.

What to remember:

  • In this worked example, every enrolled household gets a matched nonenrolled household.
  • That is a property of this sample and specification, not a general guarantee.

7.3 Effect Estimates Reported in the Export

Keep these figures in mind:

  • Matched comparison of means:
    • enrolled household health expenditures: 7.84
    • matched comparison household health expenditures: 17.79
    • estimated effect: -9.95 US dollars
  • Matched regression:
    • full explanatory set: -9.95** with standard error (0.24)
    • limited explanatory set: -11.35** with standard error (0.22)
  • Matched DiD:
    • baseline difference: 0.54
    • follow-up difference: -9.95
    • matched DiD estimate: -9.41** with standard error (0.19)

Study note:

  • The full covariate set is the more credible design in the chapter’s own logic.
  • The matched DiD result being close to the simple matched estimate is a useful consistency check inside the example.

7.4 Why the Limited Covariate Set Matters

Main idea:

  • The chapter uses a thin specification to show that “using matching” is not the same as “having a credible design.”

What this example teaches:

  • it shows why substantive knowledge of enrollment is essential
  • it warns against treating propensity-score matching as a push-button correction for selection bias

7.5 HISP Study Questions

The chapter ends by pushing the reader to ask:

  • What assumptions are needed to accept the matching estimates?
  • Why do results differ between the full and limited explanatory-variable sets?
  • How do the matching results compare with randomized assignment, and why?
  • Should HISP be scaled up nationally on this basis?

Why these questions matter:

  • They force a shift from software output to design credibility.

8 Limitations and Checklist

8.1 Limitations of Matching

Main idea:

  • The chapter is blunt that matching has serious weaknesses.

Main limits:

  • it requires large, information-rich data sets
  • it may fail because of limited common support
  • it cannot adjust for unobserved determinants of both participation and outcomes
  • the key identifying assumption about no relevant unobserved differences is strong and untestable

What to remember:

  • Matching is treated as weaker than randomized assignment, IV, and RDD.
  • The authors present it as a fallback or complement, not a first-best design.
  • Ex post matching is especially risky if baseline covariates must be reconstructed after treatment starts.

8.2 Checklist: Matching

Use this as the quick recall test:

  1. Is participation driven by unobserved variables that also affect outcomes?
  2. Are observed baseline characteristics well balanced after matching?
  3. Can a matched comparison unit be found for each treated unit, indicating adequate common support?

Quick interpretation:

  • If any of these answers look weak, the matching design is weak.

9 Implications for This Repo

  • Matching should be framed here as a design-repair tool under selection on observables, not as a generic causal estimator.
  • Any matching workflow should make baseline measurement rules explicit and refuse post-treatment covariates by default.
  • Diagnostics should be treated as core outputs, especially overlap and balance checks, not as optional appendix material.
  • When baseline outcomes are available, the repo should prefer matched DiD over matching alone.
  • Cases with a single treated unit or a small number of aggregate units are better mapped to synthetic-control logic than to household-level nearest-neighbor matching.

10 High-Level Takeaways

  • Matching can improve comparability, but it does not eliminate selection bias by itself.
  • The credibility of the design depends on baseline covariates, overlap, and knowledge of the enrollment process.
  • The chapter consistently points toward combination designs, especially matched DiD, when the data allow it.
