3 Executive Summary

  • The chapter starts with subclassification because it is the clearest way to see the logic of conditioning on observed covariates.
  • It then shows why exact stratification becomes difficult in realistic settings and uses that difficulty to motivate exact matching and then approximate matching methods.
  • A recurring theme is that all of these methods are trying to recover credible treated-control comparisons under selection on observables, but they differ in how they define similarity and how much information they discard or reweight.
  • The chapter is especially relevant to this repo because it connects directly to the repo’s teaching emphasis on design before estimation, on the curse of dimensionality, and on balance-versus-retention tradeoffs.

4 Chapter Structure and Notes

4.1 5.1 Subclassification

Main idea:

  • Subclassification is introduced as the most straightforward way to make treated and control units comparable: divide the sample into groups defined by covariates, compare treated and untreated outcomes within those groups, and then aggregate those within-group comparisons.

Why this matters:

  • This gives the reader the cleanest possible version of the matching idea before algorithms get involved.
  • It makes clear that causal adjustment is fundamentally about comparing like with like.
  • It also provides the bridge from the backdoor criterion to actual empirical practice.

Key understanding points:

  • The treatment effect is built from within-stratum comparisons rather than a single raw comparison across the whole sample.
  • The final estimate is a weighted average of stratum-level effects.
  • Subclassification works best when the relevant confounders are discrete or can be made meaningfully discrete.

What to remember:

  • This section is conceptually basic but methodologically important. If subclassification makes sense, the logic of later matching methods makes sense too.
  • Matching and weighting methods can often be read as more elaborate ways of doing what subclassification tries to do simply.

4.1.1 5.1.1 Some background

Main idea:

  • The background discussion motivates subclassification by showing how crude mean comparisons can mix treated and untreated units that are not comparable because they differ systematically on pre-treatment variables.

Why this matters:

  • This is where the chapter makes the inferential problem concrete: observed outcome differences may reflect selection into treatment rather than treatment itself.
  • Stratifying on a confounder is introduced as a direct fix for that comparability problem.

What to remember:

  • The point of stratification is not elegance. It is to remove misleading comparisons that pool unlike units.
  • This section helps anchor the whole chapter in confounding rather than in software procedure.

4.1.2 5.1.2 Identifying assumptions

Main idea:

  • The chapter ties subclassification to the identifying assumptions needed for observational causal inference, especially the idea that conditioning on the right observed covariates can block confounding pathways.

Why this matters:

  • This section is the reminder that subclassification is not mechanically causal. It is causal only if the relevant identifying assumptions hold.
  • It frames the whole chapter as an exercise in achieving conditional comparability, not magical de-biasing.

Key understanding points:

  • The central assumption is selection on observables or conditional independence after conditioning.
  • Exchangeability is being claimed within strata, not necessarily in the raw sample.
  • If important confounders are omitted, subclassification will not solve the problem.

What to remember:

  • This section is the conceptual foundation for everything that follows.
  • The later matching methods are all ways of making this identifying logic more workable in high-dimensional settings.

4.1.3 5.1.3 Subclassification exercise: Titanic data set

Main idea:

  • The Titanic example is used as a simple teaching case to show how subclassification works in practice with discrete support cells.

Why this matters:

  • It gives the reader a hands-on version of the method before the chapter moves into more technical matching procedures.
  • It also shows that subclassification is not just theory; it can be implemented transparently with a familiar dataset.

Key understanding points:

  • The example helps visualize support cells and weighted averaging across strata.
  • It shows how treatment comparisons can change materially once comparisons are made within more relevant groups.
  • It is a pedagogic stepping stone to exact matching because the logic is almost identical once strata become exact covariate profiles.

What to remember:

  • This is a good section to revisit when the later methods start to feel too abstract.
  • The repo’s titanic appendix is a natural companion to this part of the chapter.

4.1.4 5.1.4 Curse of dimensionality

Main idea:

  • The curse of dimensionality explains why simple subclassification becomes hard to use once many covariates matter at once: the sample gets split into too many cells, many cells become sparse or empty, and credible within-cell comparisons disappear.

Why this matters:

  • This is the pivot point of the chapter.
  • It explains why the simplest design logic cannot just be scaled up naïvely.
  • It motivates the move from exact stratification to exact matching and then to approximate methods.

Key understanding points:

  • Every additional covariate or finer binning choice can multiply the number of required strata.
  • A design can become unusable not because the logic is wrong, but because the data do not support the required fine-grained comparisons.
  • Approximate matching methods are responses to this sparsity problem.

What to remember:

  • The curse of dimensionality is not just a computational issue. It is a substantive overlap issue.
  • Whenever later methods relax exact comparability, this is the reason they do so.

4.2 5.2 Exact Matching

Main idea:

  • Exact matching takes the subclassification logic to its strict conclusion: treated and control units are compared only when they are identical on the chosen matching covariates.

Why this matters:

  • Exact matching is conceptually clean and easy to explain.
  • It makes the design rule explicit and minimizes ambiguity about what counts as a valid comparison.

Key understanding points:

  • Exact matching is strongest when the covariates are discrete, low-dimensional, or already naturally grouped.
  • Its main virtue is transparency: the analyst can say exactly which comparisons are allowed.
  • Its main weakness is feasibility: once the covariate set expands, many treated units may have no exact control counterpart.

What to remember:

  • Exact matching is the benchmark for interpretability even when it is not feasible.
  • If exact matching fails badly, that often reveals something important about overlap in the data rather than a mere inconvenience.

Useful caution:

  • Severe sample loss is not a side issue. It changes the target population and may turn the analysis into a very local statement about a subset with support.

4.3 5.3 Approximate Matching

Main idea:

  • Approximate matching methods relax the exact-comparison rule so the analyst can still construct usable treated-control comparisons when exact matching is too sparse.

Why this matters:

  • This is where matching becomes practically useful in real observational data.
  • The section clarifies that approximation is not abandoning the design goal; it is trying to preserve as much comparability as the data allow.

Key understanding points:

  • Different approximate methods relax the exactness requirement in different ways.
  • The central tradeoff is always between closeness of match, retained sample size, and the stability of the resulting design.
  • Approximate matching therefore requires more diagnostics and more analyst judgment than exact matching.

What to remember:

  • Approximation is necessary because the data are limited, not because causal standards are lower.
  • Every approximate method should be judged by the design it produces, not by the elegance of the algorithm.

4.3.1 5.3.1 Nearest neighbor covariate matching

Main idea:

  • Nearest-neighbor covariate matching defines similarity using a distance metric on the covariates and pairs treated units with the closest available controls.

Why this matters:

  • This is a natural generalization of exact matching when exact equality is impossible.
  • It gives a practical rule for choosing among imperfect comparison units.

Key understanding points:

  • The method depends heavily on how distance is measured.
  • “Nearest” is a relative statement, not a guarantee of good balance.
  • Match quality can vary a lot across treated units, especially where overlap is weak.

What to remember:

  • Distance-based matching is intuitive, but it can hide poor design quality if the nearest available match is still very far away.
  • Closeness has to be judged substantively and diagnostically, not assumed.

4.3.2 5.3.2 Bias correction

Main idea:

  • Bias correction is introduced because nearest-neighbor matches are rarely perfect. Even after matching, treated and control units may differ on important covariates.

Why this matters:

  • This section acknowledges that approximate matching can leave residual imbalance.
  • It shows one way analysts try to reduce the remaining bias without abandoning the matched design.

Key understanding points:

  • Bias correction is a post-match adjustment layered on top of the design.
  • It does not make poor matches good; it only tries to adjust for the remaining mismatch.
  • The need for bias correction is itself evidence that approximate matching alone may not fully solve the problem.

What to remember:

  • Bias correction can help, but it should not be confused with a cure for bad overlap.
  • The more correction the design seems to need, the weaker the underlying matched comparison may be.

4.3.3 5.3.3 Propensity score methods

Main idea:

  • Propensity score methods are presented as a way to reduce the dimensionality problem by summarizing many covariates into a single balancing score: the probability of treatment given the observed covariates.

Why this matters:

  • This is one of the chapter’s most important responses to the curse of dimensionality.
  • It explains why analysts often use propensity scores when direct matching on the full covariate vector is impractical.

Key understanding points:

  • The attraction of the propensity score is dimensional reduction, not guaranteed success.
  • Units with similar propensity scores are meant to be similar in their observed treatment-assignment profile.
  • The propensity score is useful only if it actually produces acceptable covariate balance after implementation.

What to remember:

  • The propensity score is a design device, not the estimand and not the final causal argument.
  • Good propensity score practice still requires overlap checks and balance diagnostics.

Useful caution:

  • It is easy to mistake a well-specified treatment model for a good design. The chapter’s structure implies these are not the same thing.

4.3.4 5.3.4 Example: The NSW job training program

Main idea:

  • The NSW job training example is used as a benchmark setting for assessing how well observational adjustment methods can recover effects relative to a more credible experimental reference.

Why this matters:

  • This example is central to the matching literature because it provides a way to judge whether a matching or weighting approach is actually getting close to a known benchmark.
  • It moves the discussion from abstract method comparison to empirical performance.

Key understanding points:

  • The example is not only about one program; it is about whether observational adjustment can approximate an experimental benchmark.
  • It highlights how design choices can materially affect the estimated effect.
  • It also reinforces the need to assess methods comparatively rather than doctrinally.

What to remember:

  • This is one of the strongest chapter links to current repo material because the repo already uses the NSW/CPS benchmark logic.
  • The value of the example is that it disciplines method evaluation with an external reference point.

4.3.5 5.3.5 Weighting on the propensity score

Main idea:

  • Propensity scores can be used not only for matching but also for weighting, so the adjusted sample behaves like a pseudo-population in which treatment is less confounded by observed covariates.

Why this matters:

  • This widens the chapter from pairwise or stratum-based comparisons into a broader weighting framework.
  • It also makes the link to estimands more explicit because different weights correspond to different target populations.

Key understanding points:

  • Weighting changes the influence of observations rather than necessarily dropping them.
  • Weight instability becomes a central issue when estimated treatment probabilities are near zero or one.
  • Overlap problems show up here as extreme weights rather than only as failed matches.

What to remember:

  • Weighting can preserve more units than strict matching, but it can still create fragile designs if a few observations carry too much influence.
  • Effective sample size matters as much as nominal sample size.

4.3.6 5.3.6 Nearest-neighbor matching

Main idea:

  • The chapter then treats nearest-neighbor matching in its more operational form, where design decisions such as replacement, order, and implementation details affect the resulting matched sample.

Why this matters:

  • This is where a seemingly simple idea becomes a real design workflow with choices that affect both bias and variance.
  • It reminds the reader that matching methods are not single fixed procedures.

Key understanding points:

  • Matching with replacement can improve match quality but may concentrate influence on a small number of controls.
  • Matching without replacement may spread influence more evenly but can force worse matches later in the procedure.
  • Inference depends on the structure created by the design, not only on the eventual regression or mean difference formula.

What to remember:

  • Operational choices in nearest-neighbor matching are not minor implementation details. They define the actual design.
  • Good matching is judged by the resulting balance and overlap, not by the label “nearest neighbor” alone.

4.3.7 5.3.7 Coarsened exact matching

Main idea:

  • Coarsened exact matching is presented as a compromise: exact matching is performed, but on coarsened versions of the covariates rather than on the raw covariates themselves.

Why this matters:

  • This gives the analyst direct control over what counts as meaningfully similar while avoiding the full sparsity problem of raw exact matching.
  • It is one of the clearest design-oriented methods in the chapter because its rules are explicit and interpretable.

Key understanding points:

  • Coarsening is not a technical nuisance; it is the main design choice.
  • Different cutpoints can materially change both balance and sample retention.
  • CEM often makes the balance-retention tradeoff more visible than black-box distance methods.

What to remember:

  • CEM is attractive because it keeps the matching rule understandable.
  • But it still requires iterative checking because overly fine coarsening can drop too much sample, while overly coarse binning can weaken comparability.

4.4 5.4 Conclusion

Main idea:

  • The conclusion appears to pull together subclassification, exact matching, and approximate matching as related attempts to recover credible counterfactual comparisons from observational data.

Why this matters:

  • The chapter’s ending is important because it clarifies what the methods are for and, equally, what they cannot do.

What to remember:

  • The core lesson is not that one matching method wins universally.
  • The deeper lesson is that observational adjustment is a design problem shaped by overlap, dimensionality, and identifying assumptions.
  • Matching methods are useful when they make comparisons more credible and more transparent, not when they hide weak support behind technical language.

6 High-Level Takeaways

  • Subclassification is the cleanest entry point for understanding matching because it makes the conditioning logic visible.
  • The curse of dimensionality is the main reason exact stratification breaks down in realistic settings.
  • Exact matching is transparent but often infeasible once the design involves multiple meaningful covariates.
  • Approximate matching methods differ mainly in how they relax exactness and how they manage the balance-retention tradeoff.
  • Propensity score methods solve a dimensionality problem only if they actually improve balance and maintain overlap.
  • Benchmark examples such as NSW matter because they let analysts evaluate designs against a stronger reference than intuition alone.
